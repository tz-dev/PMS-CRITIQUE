2) Minimal Vignette

2.1 Title

A training feedback scene becomes reputationally exposing rather than coordinative

2.2 Scene boundaries

  • Domain: Institutional training / onboarding / supervision / evaluative learning setting
  • Scope: Structures, role asymmetries, local exposure, evaluative framing, correction attempts, coordination gaps, critique reach, and judgment drift in a bounded institutional or small-group scene.
  • Out of scope:
    • person-level judgment
    • motive attribution
    • competence ranking
    • clinical or psychological interpretation
    • substantive verdict on whether the criticism was objectively correct
    • instruction or action prescription

2.3 Short scene

In a training session, onboarding meeting, classroom-like supervision space, or institutional feedback setting, a senior role comments on the conduct, output, or communication style of a junior or evaluated role. The mismatch is named as if it were part of developmental correction. Yet the way it is delivered makes the evaluative status difference more salient than the path toward change. Others witness the exchange, the target role bears disproportionate exposure, and the result is less a coordinated correction than a local reputational marking.

2.4 Structural focus

The example examines how critique can drift from framed mismatch into judgment when Ω is salient and Σ remains weak. The central issue is not whether the mismatch exists, but whether feedback remains interruptible and coordinative rather than becoming exposure-weighted evaluation.

2.5 Scenic elements

  • Actors:
    • trainer, supervisor, or evaluator
    • junior participant, trainee, or supervised role
    • peer group, cohort, or observing team members
    • institutional host or training frame owner
  • Salient objects:
    • the evaluated performance or behavior
    • feedback episode
    • group witnessing context
    • authority gradient
    • possible corrective next step
    • reputational residue inside the local scene
  • Trigger event:
    • Label: feedback episode under asymmetry
    • Description: A perceived mismatch is raised in a development or training frame, but the interaction begins to mark the person-position more strongly than it organizes a shared corrective path.

3) Why This Is Repo-Useful

3.1 Structural focus

This example is useful because it distinguishes productive correction from evaluative exposure. It shows how feedback can remain formally legitimate at the frame level while still drifting toward judgment when asymmetry becomes structurally dominant.

3.2 Repo value

  • Demonstrates a clean D2-type case in which evaluation replaces coordination.
  • Shows how Ω can transform a seemingly ordinary feedback scene into a locally exposing event without any publicness overlay.
  • Clarifies that feedback language alone does not guarantee critique in the strict PMS-CRITIQUE sense.
  • Helps distinguish framed mismatch from actual coordinatable correction.
  • Provides a strong repository example for how D can come under stress even in non-public, non-mass scenes.

3.3 Central repo question

When does “feedback” remain critique, and when does it become judgment under asymmetry?

3.4 Lens relevance

  • Critique thresholds: The scene clarifies the difference between naming a mismatch inside a frame and actually reaching productive critique. It often remains below CRITIQUE_PROD because Σ is weak or absent.
  • Drift typology: The case most strongly fits D2/C2: judgment masquerading as critique. It can also touch C3 where Ω rises and Χ collapses further into moralized evaluation.
  • Scale relevance: The example sits between b and c: it is not fully public, but it is more than purely inward critique because asymmetry and witnessed exposure are relationally distributed in a small institutional setting.
  • Guardrail relevance: The example invites overreading into personal failure or abusive intent, but the structural analysis must remain bounded to exposure, frame, coordination, and asymmetry.

4) Operator Mapping

4.1 Reduced signature

Primary signature: Δ, □, Ω, Θ, Χ

Secondary possible: ∇, Σ, Φ

The central signature is framed mismatch under strong role asymmetry and temporal residue. The decisive issue is whether the scene stays interruptible and coordinative or slides into exposure-weighted judgment.

4.2 Operator roles in scene

  • Δ: A mismatch is clearly named: the trainee or junior role allegedly did something insufficient, off-standard, unclear, or inappropriate within the learning frame.
  • ∇: Impulse may appear as urgency, embarrassment, defensive activation, or the evaluator’s push for immediate clarity and closure.
  • □: The training or feedback frame authorizes interruption and evaluation, and defines what counts as teachable correction or acceptable response.
  • Λ: Non-events may appear if no concrete next step, repair path, or later follow-up is given after the evaluative moment.
  • Α: A recurrent script can stabilize in which “feedback” functions mainly as local role marking: authority speaks, junior role absorbs exposure.
  • Ω: This is the decisive pressure point. The evaluator controls meaning, visibility, and status consequences more than the recipient does, so the same feedback content carries unequal exposure.
  • Θ: The episode leaves residue. Even in a local setting, one evaluative moment can harden into remembered status, hesitancy, or reputational trace.
  • Φ: Recontextualization would be present if the mismatch were translated into a concrete, scene-appropriate learning path. In this drift case, Φ is weak, partial, or subordinated to evaluation.
  • Χ: Distance is structurally at risk. The recipient may have limited stop-capability, and the evaluator may not reopen the scene for revision once the judgmental tone is set.
  • Σ: Integration is the missing or weak element. The scene often fails to consolidate a coordinatable corrective step that both roles can inhabit.
  • Ψ: Stable follow-up binding is usually absent or underdeveloped. The scene marks rather than durably supports correction.

4.3 Derived axes projection

  • A: Awareness is present insofar as a difference is noticed and sustained in frame, but awareness alone does not protect against evaluative drift.
  • C: Coherence may appear superficially strong because the evaluator produces a clear narrative of what was wrong, but that narrative may be more stabilizing than corrective.
  • R: Responsibility becomes skewed when the junior role bears the full burden of correction while the evaluator bears little of the relational cost of exposure.
  • E: Action remains weak if no coordinatable next step is integrated and the scene stops at evaluative marking.
  • D: Dignity-in-Practice is structurally stressed when asymmetry turns feedback into local exposure rather than protected correction.

4.4 Structural mechanism summary

  1. A mismatch is identified inside a legitimate learning frame.
  2. Authority gradient becomes salient during feedback delivery.
  3. Exposure concentrates on the evaluated role.
  4. Clarity is produced evaluatively rather than coordinatively.
  5. Corrective integration remains weak or absent.
  6. The scene leaves local residue and role marking.

Concise formula:
framed Δ in □ → Ω becomes salient → evaluative clarity outruns Σ → Χ weakens for the exposed role → local residue under Θ → judgment drift

10) Validity Gate Reminder

10.1 PMS entry condition

  • Χ (Distance): Distance must not be collapsed into either defensive counter-blame or evaluator certainty. Χ requires that the scene remain interruptible and revisable for all involved roles.
  • Reversibility: The analysis remains scene-bound and revisable. It does not claim that the evaluator is globally domineering or that the recipient is globally incapable.
  • D (Dignity-in-Practice): The example must not be used to shame trainers, supervisors, or trainees. It maps how asymmetry can transform feedback into exposure-weighted judgment.

10.2 Case-specific validity test

Valid if:

  • the feedback frame is acknowledged without automatically equating it with productive critique
  • Ω is tracked as structural exposure rather than moral blame
  • weak Σ is identified without reducing the scene to bad faith
  • the analysis stays bounded to role relations, correction path, and residue

Formally invalid if:

  • the example is used to prove personal abusiveness or incompetence
  • the evaluated role is redescribed as objectively deficient beyond the scene
  • drift labels become ranking devices
  • the analysis itself becomes a second-order pillory

11) Limits and Boundedness Check

11.1 Structural limits in this case

  • The analysis does not establish whether the feedback content was factually right or wrong.

13) Readable Structural Closure

In this case, a feedback scene appears corrective but functions mainly as evaluative exposure under asymmetry. The mismatch is clearly named inside a legitimate frame, yet the episode does not consolidate a shared corrective path. Instead, authority sharpens local reputational burden for the exposed role while integration remains weak. What is remembered afterward is often less the correction than the exposure.

14) Extended Scene Analysis

14.1 Frame anchor check — □

The institutional training or feedback frame is real and legitimate. It authorizes correction, which is precisely why judgment drift can hide inside it.

14.2 Trigger mismatch check — Δ

The mismatch is visible and significant enough to be addressed. The structural problem is not whether something was off, but how the handling of the mismatch is organized.

14.3 Non-event integrity check — Λ

Missing next steps, absent repair opportunities, or lack of later follow-up may reveal that feedback did not become coordinated correction.

14.4 Attractor stabilization check — Α

A stable script can emerge in which “feedback” routinely means exposure of juniors by seniors rather than shared developmental coordination.

14.5 Asymmetry audit — Ω

  • Exposure bearers:
    • the trainee or junior role receiving feedback
    • roles whose standing depends on institutional approval
    • participants who cannot easily interrupt evaluative framing
  • Lower-cost positions:
    • the evaluator or trainer
    • roles controlling interpretation and closure of the episode

14.6 Temporality and trajectory — Θ

  1. mismatch becomes visible
  2. feedback scene begins
  3. authority gradient sharpens
  4. evaluation becomes locally exposing
  5. no strong coordinative next step emerges
  6. recipient carries residue into later scenes

Reading: Θ makes the scene consequential beyond the moment itself. A brief evaluative exchange can continue structuring participation after the episode ends.

14.7 Distance availability check — Χ

Distance is unevenly distributed. The evaluator often retains more control over pacing and meaning than the exposed role, making interruptibility structurally fragile.

14.8 Integration check — Σ

Integration is the missing element. The scene does not sufficiently consolidate correction into a shared, actionable path.

14.9 Self-binding check — Ψ

Durable follow-up binding is weak. The feedback episode marks rather than binds a trajectory of supported correction.

14.10 Critique displacement statement

Core claim: The case is best read not as failed noticing, but as critique displaced into evaluative marking: the frame names what is wrong, but asymmetry turns the scene toward exposure rather than coordination.
